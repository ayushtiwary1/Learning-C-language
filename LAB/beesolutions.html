1. Define Resonance.

Definition: Resonance in an electrical circuit is a specific operating condition where the inductive reactance ($X_L$) becomes equal to the capacitive reactance ($X_C$) in magnitude ($X_L = X_C$).

Key Characteristics:

  • The net impedance of the circuit becomes purely resistive ($Z = R$).
  • The voltage and current are in phase, resulting in a Unity Power Factor.
  • In a series circuit, current is maximum; in a parallel circuit, impedance is maximum.
Medium
2. Explain resonance for series RLC circuit and derive the equation for resonant frequency.

Consider a series RLC circuit with resistance $R$, inductance $L$, and capacitance $C$ connected to an AC source $V$. The total impedance is:

$$ Z = R + j(X_L - X_C) $$

Resonance Condition: Resonance occurs when the imaginary part of the impedance is zero.

$$ X_L - X_C = 0 \Rightarrow X_L = X_C $$

Substituting the formulas for reactances ($X_L = 2\pi f L$ and $X_C = \frac{1}{2\pi f C}$):

$$ 2\pi f_r L = \frac{1}{2\pi f_r C} $$ $$ f_r^2 = \frac{1}{4\pi^2 LC} $$ $$ f_r = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}} \text{ Hz} $$

Where $f_r$ is the resonant frequency.

3. Formulate the current relationship and the voltage relationship in a 3-phase star connected system, also derive the equation for power.

Relationships in Star (Y) Connection:

  • Current: The line conductor is connected directly to the phase winding. Therefore, Line Current equals Phase Current.
    $$I_L = I_{ph}$$
  • Voltage: The line voltage is the vector difference of two phase voltages (e.g., $V_{RY} = V_R - V_Y$). Magnitude-wise:
    $$V_L = \sqrt{3}V_{ph}$$

Power Derivation:

Total Power ($P$) is the sum of power in all three phases:

$$ P = 3 \times V_{ph} \times I_{ph} \times \cos\phi $$

Substitute $V_{ph} = V_L/\sqrt{3}$ and $I_{ph} = I_L$:

$$ P = 3 \times \frac{V_L}{\sqrt{3}} \times I_L \times \cos\phi $$ $$ P = \sqrt{3} V_L I_L \cos\phi \text{ Watts} $$

4. Prove that the resultant voltage at any instant is zero in a balanced three phase system.

Let the three balanced phase voltages be:

$$ v_R = V_m \sin(\omega t) $$ $$ v_Y = V_m \sin(\omega t - 120^\circ) $$ $$ v_B = V_m \sin(\omega t - 240^\circ) $$

Summing them up:

$$ v_{sum} = V_m [ \sin\omega t + \sin(\omega t - 120^\circ) + \sin(\omega t - 240^\circ) ] $$

Using expansion $\sin(A-B) = \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B$ and values $\cos(120^\circ) = -0.5, \sin(120^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$:

$$ = V_m [ \sin\omega t + (-0.5\sin\omega t - \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\cos\omega t) + (-0.5\sin\omega t + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\cos\omega t) ] $$ $$ = V_m [ \sin\omega t - 0.5\sin\omega t - 0.5\sin\omega t ] = 0 $$

Hence proved.

5. Explain about Star and Delta connected three phase balanced circuits.

Star Connection (Y):

  • The finishing ends of all three windings are connected to a common neutral point.
  • Has 4 wires (R, Y, B, Neutral).
  • $V_L = \sqrt{3}V_{ph}$, $I_L = I_{ph}$. Used for power distribution.

Delta Connection ($\Delta$):

  • The finish of one winding connects to the start of the next, forming a closed loop.
  • Has 3 wires (no neutral).
  • $V_L = V_{ph}$, $I_L = \sqrt{3}I_{ph}$. Used for motor loads and transmission.

Unit III: Magnetic Circuits & Transformers

Easy
1. Explain the following Magnetic field properties (i) Magnetic Field (ii) Flux (iii) Flux Density (iv) Intensity (v) Permeability (vi) Reluctance (vii) Permeance (viii) MMF.
  • Magnetic Field: The space around a magnet where its influence is felt.
  • Magnetic Flux ($\phi$): The total number of magnetic lines of force. Unit: Weber (Wb).
  • Flux Density ($B$): Flux per unit area ($B=\phi/A$). Unit: Tesla (T).
  • Magnetic Intensity ($H$): The force establishing the flux ($H=NI/l$). Unit: AT/m.
  • Permeability ($\mu$): Ability to conduct flux. $\mu = \mu_0 \mu_r$.
  • Reluctance ($S$): Opposition to flux ($S=l/\mu A$). Unit: AT/Wb.
  • Permeance: Reciprocal of reluctance. Unit: Wb/AT.
  • MMF: Magneto Motive Force ($N \times I$). The driving force. Unit: Ampere-Turns.

3. Define Self Induced EMF and Mutually Induced EMF with neat sketch.

Self Induced EMF: The EMF induced in a coil due to the change of current in the coil itself. According to Lenz's law, it opposes the change. Formula: $e = -L \frac{di}{dt}$.

Mutually Induced EMF: The EMF induced in a secondary coil due to the change of current in a primary neighboring coil. Formula: $e_2 = -M \frac{di_1}{dt}$.

4. Explain B-H Curve and Draw with all parameters.

The B-H curve (Hysteresis Loop) plots Flux Density ($B$) vs Magnetic Field Intensity ($H$) for a ferromagnetic material.

  • Saturation: Point where B stops increasing with H.
  • Retentivity (Br): The flux remaining when H is reduced to zero.
  • Coercivity (Hc): The negative H required to reduce B to zero.
  • Hysteresis Loss: The area of the loop represents energy loss as heat.

5. A nonmagnetic ring has a mean diameter of 44.5cm and a cross sectional area of $12cm^{2}$. It is uniformly wound with 500 turns. Calculate the Field strength and Total flux produced in the ring by a current of 1A.

Given: $D=0.445m$, $A=12 \times 10^{-4}m^2$, $N=500$, $I=1A$, Non-magnetic ($\mu_r=1$).

Step 1: Mean Length ($l$)

$$ l = \pi \times D = \pi \times 0.445 = 1.398 \, m $$

Step 2: Field Strength ($H$)

$$ H = \frac{NI}{l} = \frac{500 \times 1}{1.398} = \mathbf{357.65 \, AT/m} $$

Step 3: Flux Density ($B$)

$$ B = \mu_0 H = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 357.65 = 4.49 \times 10^{-4} \, T $$

Step 4: Total Flux ($\phi$)

$$ \phi = B \times A = 4.49 \times 10^{-4} \times 12 \times 10^{-4} = \mathbf{0.539 \, \mu Wb} $$

7. Why transformers are rated in kVA?

Transformer losses consist of:

  1. Iron Losses: Depend on Voltage ($V$).
  2. Copper Losses: Depend on Current ($I$).

The total heating (loss) depends on the product of Volts and Amperes ($VA$), not on the power factor ($\cos\phi$) of the load. Since the manufacturer does not know what load power factor the user will connect, the rating is given in kVA, not kW.

8. Explain the construction of a magnetic core of a transformer.

The core provides a low reluctance path for magnetic flux. It is made of Cold Rolled Grain Oriented (CRGO) Silicon Steel.

  • Lamination: The core is built of thin laminations (0.35mm to 0.5mm) insulated from each other by varnish to minimize Eddy Current Losses.
  • Silicon Steel: Used to minimize Hysteresis Losses.

A single-phase transformer consists of:

  2. Windings: Primary and Secondary coils made of copper. Placed on the limbs.

11. Formulate the EMF equation for Transformer.

Let flux $\phi = \phi_m \sin(\omega t)$.

Induced EMF per turn $e = -\frac{d\phi}{dt} = -\frac{d}{dt}(\phi_m \sin \omega t) = -\omega \phi_m \cos \omega t = \omega \phi_m \sin(\omega t - 90^\circ)$.

Maximum value $E_{max} = \omega \phi_m = 2\pi f \phi_m$.

RMS value = $E_{max} / \sqrt{2} = \frac{2\pi f \phi_m}{\sqrt{2}} = 4.44 f \phi_m$.

Equation:

$$ E_1 = 4.44 f N_1 \phi_m $$ $$ E_2 = 4.44 f N_2 \phi_m $$

12. Examine the meaning of transformer regulation.

Voltage Regulation is defined as the change in the secondary terminal voltage from No-load to Full-load, expressed as a percentage of the no-load voltage (or rated voltage), assuming the primary voltage remains constant.

$$ \% Reg = \frac{V_{no-load} - V_{full-load}}{V_{no-load}} \times 100 $$

It indicates how much the voltage drops when a load is connected.

Medium
13. Express the condition for maximum efficiency for a transformer.

Efficiency $\eta = \frac{Output}{Output + Losses}$.

Losses = Constant Iron Loss ($P_i$) + Variable Copper Loss ($I^2 R_{eq}$).

Differentiating efficiency with respect to load current $I$ and equating to zero yields the condition:

Variable Copper Loss = Constant Iron Loss

$$ I^2 R_{eq} = P_i $$

Load at max efficiency: $kVA_{max} = kVA_{rated} \times \sqrt{\frac{P_i}{P_{cu(FL)}}}$.

14. What is meant by losses in a transformer? And give the list of losses.

Losses refer to the power wasted inside the transformer, reducing efficiency. Types:

  1. Core (Iron) Losses: Constant losses.
    • Hysteresis Loss
    • Eddy Current Loss
  2. Copper (Ohmic) Losses: $I^2R$ heat loss in windings. Varies with load.
  3. Stray Load Losses: Minor losses in tank/structure due to leakage flux.

  • Core Type: The windings surround a considerable part of the core. Has 2 limbs. Flux has a single path. Better for High Voltage (easier insulation).
  • Shell Type: The core surrounds a considerable part of the windings. Has 3 limbs. Flux splits into two paths. Better mechanical strength, used for Low Voltage, High Current.

16. Formulate expression for step up/down transformer according to transformation ratio.

Transformation Ratio ($K$):

$$ K = \frac{N_2}{N_1} = \frac{E_2}{E_1} $$
  • If $K > 1$ ($N_2 > N_1$): Step-up Transformer (Output Voltage > Input Voltage).
  • If $K < 1$ ($N_2 < N_1$): Step-down Transformer (Output Voltage < Input Voltage).

Open Circuit (O.C.) Test:

  • HV side is left Open. Instruments connected to LV side.
  • Rated Voltage applied to LV.
  • Measures: Core (Iron) Losses ($W_0$) and No-load parameters ($R_0, X_0$).

  • LV side is Shorted with a thick wire. Instruments connected to HV side.
  • Low Voltage (variable) applied to HV until Rated Current flows.
  • Measures: Full Load Copper Losses ($W_{sc}$) and Equivalent Impedance ($Z_{eq}, R_{eq}, X_{eq}$).

Unit IV: DC & Induction Motors

Easy
1. Write the principle of DC Motor?

Principle: When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a mechanical force given by Lorentz force law ($F = BIl \sin\theta$). Since the conductors are on the armature periphery, this force creates a torque that causes rotation. The direction is given by Fleming's Left Hand Rule.

  • Shunt Motor: Lathes, Centrifugal pumps, Fans (Constant speed applications).
  • Series Motor: Electric traction, Cranes, Hoists (High starting torque required).
  • Compound Motor: Rolling mills, Presses (High starting torque with speed limit).

3. With a neat circuit diagram Explain the construction and principle of operation of DC Motor and explain the different parts.

Construction Parts:

  • Yoke: Outer frame, protects and carries magnetic flux.
  • Pole Cores & Shoes: Electromagnets to produce field flux.
  • Armature Core: Rotating part, laminated, carries slots for conductors.

6. Explain back emf in a DC motor.

When the armature rotates in the magnetic field, conductors cut flux and an EMF is induced according to Faraday's law. By Lenz's law, this EMF opposes the applied voltage $V$ that causes the rotation. Hence, it is called Back EMF ($E_b$).

$$ V = E_b + I_a R_a $$

Significance: It makes the DC motor a self-regulating machine. If load increases, speed drops, $E_b$ drops, allowing more $I_a$ to flow to handle the load.

7. Distinguish the difference between DC Motor and DC Generator.
  • Energy Conversion: Motor converts Electrical to Mechanical. Generator converts Mechanical to Electrical.
  • EMF Direction: In Motor, induced EMF opposes current ($V > E_b$). In Generator, induced EMF drives current ($E_g > V$).
  • Rule: Motor uses Fleming's Left Hand Rule. Generator uses Right Hand Rule.

9. Criticize "belt drive not suitable for DC Series Motor why?"

In a DC Series Motor, speed is inversely proportional to flux ($N \propto 1/\phi$). Since $\phi \propto I_a$, at no load, current is very small, flux is negligible, and speed becomes dangerously high ($N \to \infty$).

If a belt drive breaks, the load drops to zero, and the motor will race to destructive speeds, potentially causing the armature to burst due to centrifugal force. Hence, direct coupling or gears are used.

10. Mention the effects of differential compounding and cumulatively compound on the performance of DC Compound motor.
  • Cumulative Compound: Series flux aids shunt flux ($\phi_{total} = \phi_{sh} + \phi_{se}$). Provides high starting torque (like series) but has a definite no-load speed (like shunt). Used for intermittent high loads.
  • Differential Compound: Series flux opposes shunt flux ($\phi_{total} = \phi_{sh} - \phi_{se}$). As load increases, flux decreases, and speed tries to increase. This leads to instability. Rarely used.

11. Discuss why starting current is high at the moment of starting a DC Motor? Methods of limiting it.

Reason: $I_a = \frac{V - E_b}{R_a}$. At start, speed $N=0$, so Back EMF $E_b=0$. Thus $I_{start} = V/R_a$. Since armature resistance $R_a$ is very small, the starting current is dangerously high (5-7 times rated current).

Methods to Limit:

  • 3-Point Starter: Inserts external resistance in series with armature during starting.
  • 4-Point Starter: Similar to 3-point but handles speed control by field weakening better.

25. Derive the torque-slip equation for a 3-phase induction motor and equation for slip at max torque.

Rotor Current $I_2 = \frac{s E_2}{\sqrt{R_2^2 + (s X_2)^2}}$.

Torque $T \propto \text{Rotor Input Power} / N_s$. Input Power = $3 I_2^2 R_2 / s$.

Differentiating w.r.t slip $s$ and equating to 0 gives condition $R_2 = s X_2$.

Slip at max torque $s_{max} = R_2 / X_2$.

26. How is the speed of a 3-phase induction motor controlled by its stator voltage control?

Torque is proportional to the square of the voltage ($T \propto sV^2$).

If supply voltage $V$ is reduced, the developed torque decreases. To drive the same constant load torque, the motor must increase its slip (slow down). Thus, speed decreases.

Limitation: Range is limited as efficiency drops and overheating risks increase.
