Descriptive Questions

Q.1 What is Laser?

LASER stands for Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. It is a device that produces a highly intense, monochromatic, coherent, and directional beam of light.

Q.2 What are Einstein's coefficients?

Einstein introduced three probability coefficients to quantify the interaction of radiation with matter:

  • Absorption Coefficient ($B_{12}$): Probability of an atom absorbing a photon to jump to a higher state per unit energy density.
  • Spontaneous Emission Coefficient ($A_{21}$): Probability of an atom falling to a lower state and emitting a photon naturally (randomly) per unit time.
  • Stimulated Emission Coefficient ($B_{21}$): Probability of an incident photon triggering an excited atom to drop to a lower state, emitting a clone photon.

Q.3 What is a gas laser?

A gas laser is a type of laser where the active medium is a gas or a mixture of gases. An electric discharge is usually used to pump the gas atoms to excited states. Common examples include the Helium-Neon (He-Ne) laser and the Carbon Dioxide ($\text{CO}_2$) laser.

Q.4 What is stimulated emission?

Stimulated emission is the process where an incoming photon of specific energy interacts with an excited atom, forcing it to drop to a lower energy state. This releases a second photon identical to the first in phase, frequency, direction, and polarization. This is the fundamental principle behind laser amplification.

Q.5 Write the differences between spontaneous and stimulated emission.

Spontaneous Emission Stimulated Emission
Occurs naturally without external trigger. Requires an external photon to trigger it.
Emitted light is incoherent (random phase). Emitted light is highly coherent (same phase).
Photons travel in random directions. Photons travel in a specific direction.
Example: Light from a bulb. Example: Laser light.

Q.6 What is meant by population inversion and how is it achieved?

  • Definition: Population inversion is a non-equilibrium state where the number of atoms in a higher energy (excited) state ($N_2$) is greater than the number of atoms in the lower energy (ground) state ($N_1$). Mathematically: $$N_2 > N_1$$
  • Achievement: It is achieved through a process called Pumping, which supplies external energy (optical, electrical, or chemical) to raise atoms to the excited state against the natural thermal equilibrium.

Q.7 What are the properties of the laser beam?

  1. Monochromaticity: It contains only one specific wavelength (color).
  2. Coherence: All waves are in phase with each other spatially and temporally.
  3. Directionality: The beam travels in a straight line with very little divergence.
  4. High Intensity: The energy is concentrated in a very narrow beam.

Q.8 What is optical resonant cavity?

An optical resonant cavity (or resonator) consists of a pair of mirrors placed around the active medium. One mirror is fully reflective ($100\%$), and the other is partially reflective (e.g., $99\%$). It reflects light back and forth through the medium to build up amplification via stimulated emission before the beam emerges through the partial reflector.

Q.9 What is Nd-YAG laser?

It is a solid-state laser with the following characteristics:

  • Active Medium: Yttrium Aluminum Garnet (YAG) crystal doped with Neodymium ($\text{Nd}^{3+}$) ions.
  • Pumping: Optical pumping (usually using a flash lamp or laser diode).
  • Output: It emits infrared light at a wavelength of $1064 \text{ nm}$ ($1.064 \mu\text{m}$).

Q.10 What is a semiconductor laser?

Also known as a Laser Diode, it uses a p-n junction of a direct bandgap semiconductor (like Gallium Arsenide, GaAs) as the active medium. When forward biased, electron-hole recombination at the junction releases photons, generating laser light.

Q.11 What are the applications of semiconductor laser?

  • Fiber optic communications.
  • Barcode scanners.
  • Laser printers.
  • CD/DVD/Blu-ray players.

Q.12 Describe He-Ne gas laser with proper diagram.

Construction:

It consists of a quartz tube filled with a mixture of Helium and Neon gas (ratio 10:1) at low pressure. It features two mirrors forming an optical resonator and electrodes connected to a high-voltage power supply.

Working Mechanism:

  1. Electric discharge excites Helium atoms to metastable states ($2^1s$ and $2^3s$).
  2. He atoms transfer energy to Neon atoms via Resonant Energy Transfer collisions.
  3. Neon atoms achieve population inversion.
  4. Stimulated emission occurs between Neon levels, emitting red laser light at $632.8 \text{ nm}$.

Q.13 Describe Ruby laser and its working.

  • Active Medium: A synthetic ruby rod ($\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3$ doped with roughly 0.05% $\text{Cr}^{3+}$ ions).
  • Pumping Source: Optical pumping using a helical Xenon flash lamp.
  • Working: The flash lamp emits intense white light, exciting Chromium ions from the ground state to absorption bands. They rapidly decay to a metastable state. When population inversion is reached, stimulated emission produces a pulsed red beam at $694.3 \text{ nm}$. It operates as a three-level laser system.

Q.14 What do you understand by three-level and four-level pumping?

Three-Level System

Atoms are pumped from Ground (1) to Pump level (3), decay to Metastable (2), and lase back to Ground (1). Since the lower laser level is the ground state (which is full), very high pump power is required to achieve inversion ($N_2 > N_1$).
(e.g., Ruby Laser)

Four-Level System

Atoms lase between two excited states ($3 \to 2$). The lower laser level (2) is effectively empty (rapid decay to ground). This makes it much easier to maintain population inversion with less energy.
(e.g., He-Ne, Nd:YAG)

Q.15 Derive the ratio of rate of stimulated emission and absorption in thermal equilibrium.

At thermal equilibrium, the rate of upward transitions (absorption) equals the rate of downward transitions (spontaneous + stimulated emission). Focusing on the driven processes:

Rate of Absorption: $$R_{abs} = B_{12} u(\nu) N_1$$

Rate of Stimulated Emission: $$R_{stim} = B_{21} u(\nu) N_2$$

Since Einstein proved that the probability coefficients are equal ($B_{12} = B_{21}$), the ratio of the rates depends only on the populations:

$$ \frac{R_{stim}}{R_{abs}} = \frac{B_{21} u(\nu) N_2}{B_{12} u(\nu) N_1} = \frac{N_2}{N_1} $$

Using Boltzmannâ€™s Law ($N_2 = N_1 e^{-h\nu/k_B T}$):

$$ \frac{R_{stim}}{R_{abs}} = e^{-h\nu / k_B T} $$

Q.16 What are the required conditions to achieve laser light?

  1. Metastable State: The active medium must possess a long-lived excited state to allow population accumulation.
  2. Population Inversion: The number of atoms in the excited state must exceed those in the lower state ($N_2 > N_1$).
  3. Stimulated Emission Dominance: The probability of stimulated emission must be higher than spontaneous emission or absorption.
  4. Optical Feedback: An optical resonator (mirrors) is required to reflect light back for amplification.

Q.2 What is the need to achieve population inversion?

Correct Option: (a) To excite most of the atoms

Explanation: This ensures $N_{excited} > N_{ground}$, making stimulated emission the dominant process.

Q.3 Laser beam is made of:

Correct Option: (d) None of them

Explanation: Lasers are made of photons (light quanta). Electrons, protons, and phonons are incorrect.

Q.4 The lifetime of electrons in metastable state is of the order of:

Correct Option: (b) $10^{-3}$ s

Explanation: Normal excited states last $\approx 10^{-8}$ s, whereas metastable states last significantly longer ($\approx 10^{-3}$ s).

Correct Option: (a) He-Ne laser

Explanation: Ruby and Nd-YAG are solid-state lasers; Semiconductor is a diode laser. He-Ne uses Helium and Neon gas.

Correct Option: (a) Molecular collision

Explanation: Specifically, inelastic resonant atom-atom collisions between excited Helium and ground-state Neon atoms.
