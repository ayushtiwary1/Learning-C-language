1. Explain the following Magnetic field properties (i) Magnetic Field (ii) Flux (iii) Flux Density (iv) Intensity (v) Permeability (vi) Reluctance (vii) Permeance (viii) MMF.
  • Magnetic Field: The space around a magnet where its influence is felt.
  • Magnetic Flux ($\phi$): The total number of magnetic lines of force. Unit: Weber (Wb).
  • Flux Density ($B$): Flux per unit area ($B=\phi/A$). Unit: Tesla (T).
  • Magnetic Intensity ($H$): The force establishing the flux ($H=NI/l$). Unit: AT/m.
  • Permeability ($\mu$): Ability to conduct flux. $\mu = \mu_0 \mu_r$.
  • Reluctance ($S$): Opposition to flux ($S=l/\mu A$). Unit: AT/Wb.
  • Permeance: Reciprocal of reluctance. Unit: Wb/AT.
  • MMF: Magneto Motive Force ($N \times I$). The driving force. Unit: Ampere-Turns.

4. Explain B-H Curve and Draw with all parameters.

The B-H curve (Hysteresis Loop) plots Flux Density ($B$) vs Magnetic Field Intensity ($H$) for a ferromagnetic material.

  • Saturation: Point where B stops increasing with H.
  • Retentivity (Br): The flux remaining when H is reduced to zero.
  • Coercivity (Hc): The negative H required to reduce B to zero.
  • Hysteresis Loss: The area of the loop represents energy loss as heat.

5. A nonmagnetic ring has a mean diameter of 44.5cm and a cross sectional area of $12cm^{2}$. It is uniformly wound with 500 turns. Calculate the Field strength and Total flux produced in the ring by a current of 1A.

Given: $D=0.445m$, $A=12 \times 10^{-4}m^2$, $N=500$, $I=1A$, Non-magnetic ($\mu_r=1$).

Step 1: Mean Length ($l$)

$$ l = \pi \times D = \pi \times 0.445 = 1.398 \, m $$

Step 2: Field Strength ($H$)

$$ H = \frac{NI}{l} = \frac{500 \times 1}{1.398} = \mathbf{357.65 \, AT/m} $$

Step 3: Flux Density ($B$)

$$ B = \mu_0 H = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 357.65 = 4.49 \times 10^{-4} \, T $$

Step 4: Total Flux ($\phi$)

$$ \phi = B \times A = 4.49 \times 10^{-4} \times 12 \times 10^{-4} = \mathbf{0.539 \, \mu Wb} $$

6. State the principle of operation of a transformer.

A transformer operates on the principle of Mutual Induction between two magnetically coupled coils. When an alternating voltage is applied to the primary winding, it creates an alternating magnetic flux in the core. This flux links with the secondary winding, inducing an EMF in it according to Faraday’s Laws of Electromagnetic Induction.

Easy
7. Why transformers are rated in kVA?

Transformer losses consist of:

  1. Iron Losses: Depend on Voltage ($V$).
  2. Copper Losses: Depend on Current ($I$).

The total heating (loss) depends on the product of Volts and Amperes ($VA$), not on the power factor ($\cos\phi$) of the load. Since the manufacturer does not know what load power factor the user will connect, the rating is given in kVA, not kW.

11. Formulate the EMF equation for Transformer.

Let flux $\phi = \phi_m \sin(\omega t)$.

Induced EMF per turn $e = -\frac{d\phi}{dt} = -\frac{d}{dt}(\phi_m \sin \omega t) = -\omega \phi_m \cos \omega t = \omega \phi_m \sin(\omega t - 90^\circ)$.

Maximum value $E_{max} = \omega \phi_m = 2\pi f \phi_m$.

RMS value = $E_{max} / \sqrt{2} = \frac{2\pi f \phi_m}{\sqrt{2}} = 4.44 f \phi_m$.

Equation:

$$ E_1 = 4.44 f N_1 \phi_m $$ $$ E_2 = 4.44 f N_2 \phi_m $$
Medium
13. Express the condition for maximum efficiency for a transformer.

Efficiency $\eta = \frac{Output}{Output + Losses}$.

Losses = Constant Iron Loss ($P_i$) + Variable Copper Loss ($I^2 R_{eq}$).

Differentiating efficiency with respect to load current $I$ and equating to zero yields the condition:

Variable Copper Loss = Constant Iron Loss

$$ I^2 R_{eq} = P_i $$

Load at max efficiency: $kVA_{max} = kVA_{rated} \times \sqrt{\frac{P_i}{P_{cu(FL)}}}$.

Easy
14. What is meant by losses in a transformer? And give the list of losses.

Losses refer to the power wasted inside the transformer, reducing efficiency. Types:

  1. Core (Iron) Losses: Constant losses.
    • Hysteresis Loss
    • Eddy Current Loss
  2. Copper (Ohmic) Losses: $I^2R$ heat loss in windings. Varies with load.
  3. Stray Load Losses: Minor losses in tank/structure due to leakage flux.

16. Formulate expression for step up/down transformer according to transformation ratio.

Transformation Ratio ($K$):

$$ K = \frac{N_2}{N_1} = \frac{E_2}{E_1} $$
  • If $K > 1$ ($N_2 > N_1$): Step-up Transformer (Output Voltage > Input Voltage).
  • If $K < 1$ ($N_2 < N_1$): Step-down Transformer (Output Voltage < Input Voltage).

18. Procedure for conducting S.C. and O.C. test on a single-phase transformer.

Open Circuit (O.C.) Test:

  • HV side is left Open. Instruments connected to LV side.
  • Rated Voltage applied to LV.
  • Measures: Core (Iron) Losses ($W_0$) and No-load parameters ($R_0, X_0$).

Short Circuit (S.C.) Test:

  • LV side is Shorted with a thick wire. Instruments connected to HV side.
  • Low Voltage (variable) applied to HV until Rated Current flows.
  • Measures: Full Load Copper Losses ($W_{sc}$) and Equivalent Impedance ($Z_{eq}, R_{eq}, X_{eq}$).

Unit IV: DC & Induction Motors

Easy
1. Write the principle of DC Motor?

Principle: When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a mechanical force given by Lorentz force law ($F = BIl \sin\theta$). Since the conductors are on the armature periphery, this force creates a torque that causes rotation. The direction is given by Fleming's Left Hand Rule.

Medium
3. With a neat circuit diagram Explain the construction and principle of operation of DC Motor and explain the different parts.

Construction Parts:

  • Yoke: Outer frame, protects and carries magnetic flux.
  • Pole Cores & Shoes: Electromagnets to produce field flux.
  • Armature Core: Rotating part, laminated, carries slots for conductors.
  • Commutator: Split copper rings, converts AC (induced) to DC (external) or vice versa.
  • Brushes: Collect current from rotating commutator.

Operation: DC supply -> Current in Armature -> Force on conductors (F=BIL) -> Torque -> Rotation.

4. Define Torque and derive the expression for torque in a D.C. Motor.

Torque ($T$): The turning moment of a force.

Derivation:

Electrical Power converted to Mechanical Power: $P_m = E_b I_a$.

Mechanical Power in terms of torque: $P_m = T \times \omega = T \times \frac{2\pi N}{60}$.

Equating both: $T \times \frac{2\pi N}{60} = E_b I_a$.

Substitute Back EMF equation $E_b = \frac{\phi Z N P}{60 A}$:

$$ T \times \frac{2\pi N}{60} = \left(\frac{\phi Z N P}{60 A}\right) I_a $$ $$ T = \frac{\phi Z P I_a}{2\pi A} = 0.159 \phi Z I_a \left(\frac{P}{A}\right) \, Nm $$
Easy
6. Explain back emf in a DC motor.

When the armature rotates in the magnetic field, conductors cut flux and an EMF is induced according to Faraday's law. By Lenz's law, this EMF opposes the applied voltage $V$ that causes the rotation. Hence, it is called Back EMF ($E_b$).

$$ V = E_b + I_a R_a $$

Significance: It makes the DC motor a self-regulating machine. If load increases, speed drops, $E_b$ drops, allowing more $I_a$ to flow to handle the load.

12. A 460V DC Series Motor runs at 1000 rpm calculate the speed and percentage change in torque if the load is reduced so that the motor is taking 30A. Total resistances 0.8Ω. Assume flux is proportional to field current.

Issue: Initial current ($I_1$) is missing. Assuming standard problem context where rated current is higher, e.g., let's assume $I_1 = 50A$ for calculation purposes.

Given: $V=460V, R=0.8\Omega, N_1=1000$. Case 2: $I_2=30A$. Flux $\phi \propto I$.

Step 1: Calculate Back EMFs $$ E_{b1} = V - I_1 R = 460 - (50 \times 0.8) = 420 \, V $$ $$ E_{b2} = V - I_2 R = 460 - (30 \times 0.8) = 436 \, V $$ Step 2: Speed Calculation

Relation: $N \propto \frac{E_b}{\phi} \propto \frac{E_b}{I}$.

$$ \frac{N_2}{N_1} = \frac{E_{b2}}{E_{b1}} \times \frac{I_1}{I_2} $$ $$ N_2 = 1000 \times \frac{436}{420} \times \frac{50}{30} = \mathbf{1730 \, rpm} $$ Step 3: Torque Change

$T \propto \phi I \propto I^2$.

$$ \frac{T_2}{T_1} = \left(\frac{30}{50}\right)^2 = 0.36 $$

Percentage change = $(1 - 0.36) \times 100 = \mathbf{64\% \text{ reduction}}$.

21. Give the conditions for maximum torque for 3-phase induction motor?

Maximum torque occurs when the Rotor Resistance ($R_2$) equals the Rotor Reactance at that slip ($sX_2$).

$$ R_2 = s_m X_2 $$

Slip at max torque $s_m = R_2 / X_2$.

Medium
22. Derive a relation between full load torque and maximum torque of a three phase Induction Motor.

Torque Equation: $T \propto \frac{s R_2}{R_2^2 + (sX_2)^2}$.

Let $a = R_2/X_2 = s_m$ (slip at max torque).

Ratio:

$$ \frac{T_{fl}}{T_{max}} = \frac{2 s_{fl} s_m}{s_{fl}^2 + s_m^2} $$

Where $s_{fl}$ is full load slip.

Easy
24. Define slip and its condition at the time of starting of 3-phase IM.

Slip ($s$): The difference between synchronous speed ($N_s$) and rotor speed ($N$) expressed as a fraction of synchronous speed.

$$ s = \frac{N_s - N}{N_s} $$

At Starting: The rotor is stationary, so $N = 0$. Therefore, $s = \frac{N_s - 0}{N_s} = 1$.

Medium
25. Derive the torque-slip equation for a 3-phase induction motor and equation for slip at max torque.

Rotor Current $I_2 = \frac{s E_2}{\sqrt{R_2^2 + (s X_2)^2}}$.

Torque $T \propto \text{Rotor Input Power} / N_s$. Input Power = $3 I_2^2 R_2 / s$.

$$ T = \frac{3}{2\pi N_s} \times \frac{s E_2^2 R_2}{R_2^2 + (sX_2)^2} $$

Differentiating w.r.t slip $s$ and equating to 0 gives condition $R_2 = s X_2$.

Slip at max torque $s_{max} = R_2 / X_2$.
